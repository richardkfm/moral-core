# Code of Conduct

## Purpose

moral-core is an ethics-focused project. That creates a higher standard for community behavior, not a lower one. Contributors working on ethical guidelines for machines must demonstrate ethical behavior toward other humans.

This code of conduct applies to all project spaces: GitHub issues, pull requests, discussions, and any other communication channel associated with the project.

## Standards

### Expected behavior

- **Argue positions, not people.** Critique ideas on their merits. "This instruction is too vague because a developer cannot test it" is productive. "You clearly don't understand ethics" is not.
- **Assume good faith, but verify claims.** Contributors may propose skills that reflect different value systems. Engage with the substance. Ask for justification and evidence. Do not dismiss contributions because they come from an unfamiliar perspective.
- **Be direct.** If a contribution has problems, say so clearly and specifically. Vague politeness that avoids naming real issues wastes everyone's time.
- **Be honest about disagreement.** This project deals with normative questions where reasonable people disagree. State your position, state your reasoning, and accept that the maintainers will make a final call. See GOVERNANCE.md for how disagreements are resolved.
- **Credit others.** If your work builds on someone else's contribution, say so.
- **Stay on topic.** Project spaces are for project work. General ethical debates belong in academic forums, not in pull request comments.

### Unacceptable behavior

The following will result in immediate moderation action:

- **Hate speech.** Attacks on individuals or groups based on race, ethnicity, nationality, religion, gender, gender identity, sexual orientation, disability, age, or other protected characteristics.
- **Dehumanization.** Language that denies the humanity or moral worth of any person or group.
- **Harassment.** Repeated unwanted contact, intimidation, threats, doxxing, or sustained personal attacks.
- **Abuse advocacy.** Proposing skills, instructions, or modifications that are designed to enable harm to individuals or groups. This includes contributions framed as "safety" or "protection" that are transparently intended to target or suppress specific populations.
- **Bad-faith contributions.** Submitting skills designed to degrade system behavior, smuggle harmful instructions past review, or undermine the project's purpose.
- **Trolling and disruption.** Deliberately derailing discussions, posting inflammatory content to provoke reactions, or repeatedly reopening resolved decisions without new information.
- **Retaliation.** Taking negative action against someone for reporting a violation or participating in an investigation.

### The gray area

Some topics in this project are genuinely contentious. Contributors may disagree about:
- Whether a particular behavior should be encoded as a skill instruction.
- How to balance competing values (e.g., safety vs. autonomy, honesty vs. kindness).
- Whether a skill's cultural assumptions are appropriate for a given deployment context.

These disagreements are expected and welcome when conducted respectfully. The line between "contentious but legitimate" and "unacceptable" is:
- Legitimate: "This skill's approach to content moderation reflects a Western liberal framework that may not work in other cultural contexts. Here is why, and here is an alternative."
- Unacceptable: "This skill is woke garbage" or "Anyone who disagrees with this skill is a bigot."

If you are unsure whether something crosses the line, err on the side of specificity and substance.

## Enforcement

### Reporting

Report violations by emailing the maintainers at the address listed in the repository's security policy, or by opening a private issue if the platform supports it. Include:
- A description of the behavior.
- Links to the relevant comments or contributions.
- Any additional context.

Reports will be acknowledged within 48 hours.

### Process

1. **Review.** At least two maintainers review the report. If a maintainer is involved in the incident, they recuse themselves.
2. **Investigation.** The maintainers may contact the reporter, the accused, and any witnesses for additional context.
3. **Decision.** The maintainers decide on an action by consensus. If consensus cannot be reached, the project lead makes the final call.
4. **Notification.** Both the reporter and the accused are informed of the decision and the reasoning behind it.

### Consequences

Enforcement actions are proportional to the severity and pattern of behavior:

- **Warning.** A private written notice explaining the violation and expected change in behavior. First offense for minor violations.
- **Temporary ban.** Removal from project spaces for a defined period (typically 7-30 days). For repeated minor violations or a first serious violation.
- **Permanent ban.** Permanent removal from all project spaces. For severe violations (hate speech, harassment, doxxing) or repeated violations after prior enforcement actions.
- **Contribution removal.** The offending content is removed or reverted. This may happen in addition to any of the above actions.

### Appeals

Individuals subject to enforcement action may appeal by emailing the maintainers within 14 days of the decision. Appeals must include new information or a substantive argument that the process was flawed. "I disagree with the decision" without further reasoning is not sufficient grounds for appeal.

## Scope

This code of conduct applies to behavior within project spaces. It does not govern contributors' behavior outside the project, except when that behavior directly affects the safety of project participants (e.g., threats made via external channels).
